# CORE.md — Capital Flow Dynamics & Multipolar Rebalancing

*Theory Package: capital_flows*
*Stability Class: cyclical*

---

## theory_id

`capital_flows`

---

## core_claim

Capital flows between developed and emerging markets in cyclical rotations driven by the interaction of valuation gaps and dollar direction. The dollar is the gravity field: when it weakens, capital rotates from expensive developed markets to cheap emerging markets; when it strengthens, capital returns to developed markets regardless of EM valuations. The rotation mechanism is reflexive — capital flowing into EM strengthens EM currencies, which eases EM financial conditions, which attracts more capital. The cycle runs 2–5 years once catalysts confirm, then reverses as multiples normalise and dollar dynamics shift.

---

## causal_mechanism

### Chain of Cause and Effect

1. **Valuation gap builds.** Extended developed-market outperformance compresses EM multiples and inflates DM multiples. Passive index flows compound the gap (market-cap-weighted indices funnel capital to the largest market, currently the US). A strong dollar discourages EM allocation by creating currency headwinds. EM-specific events (property crises, regulatory shifts, commodity downturns) depress EM prices further. The gap is a necessary but not sufficient condition for rotation.

2. **Catalyst arrives.** The dollar weakens. Possible triggers include: DM growth disappointing, fiscal dominance eroding dollar confidence, EM growth accelerating (closing the relative growth gap), or geopolitical diversification reducing structural dollar demand. The specific trigger matters less than the fact of sustained dollar weakness. Once the dollar turns, everything downstream follows.

3. **Dollar weakness eases EM financial conditions.** Dollar-denominated debt (approximately $13T outstanding outside the US, per BIS data) becomes cheaper to service in local currency. Commodity export revenues increase in local currency terms. Currency mismatch risk in EM banking systems declines. EM central banks gain room to ease domestically because they no longer need to defend currencies against dollar strength.

4. **China credit impulse transmits globally.** When China eases credit, the transmission follows a roughly sequential pattern: domestic demand recovers first (0–6 months), global commodity prices rise next (6–9 months), EM earnings revisions turn positive (9–12 months), and portfolio flows begin (12–18 months). China is the swing variable for global EM because it is the single largest driver of commodity demand, Asian supply chain activity, and EM fund weightings.

5. **Reflexive loop activates.** Capital flowing into EM strengthens EM currencies, which eases EM financial conditions further, which accelerates EM growth, which attracts more capital. Simultaneously, capital flowing out of DM causes relative underperformance and dollar weakness, reinforcing the rotation. The loop is self-reinforcing until it exhausts. Historical duration: 2–5 years.

6. **Regional sequencing.** China moves first and most aggressively in every historical EM rotation. Broad EM follows with a 2–6 month lag. India participates but has its own domestic cycle and can outperform independently of the China-driven rotation. European exporters with significant EM revenue exposure benefit as a derivative play. Japan benefits from Asian growth recovery but currency dynamics complicate returns for dollar-based investors.

7. **Rotation exhausts and reverses.** EM multiples normalise (the PE gap narrows). Dollar stabilises or re-strengthens (US growth reaccelerates or EM overheats). China credit impulse turns negative as policy normalises. Capital rotates back to DM. The cycle resets to the accumulation phase.

### The Dollar as Gravity Field

The dollar deserves special emphasis because it is the single most important variable in this theory. Valuation gaps, China credit impulse, and commodity prices are all downstream of or conditioned on dollar direction.

Why the dollar functions as gravity:

**Dollar-denominated debt.** When the dollar strengthens, approximately $13T of non-US dollar debt becomes more expensive to service in local currency. This is a tightening of global financial conditions that the Fed does not control and may not intend. Dollar weakness is the reverse — a loosening that is arguably more powerful for global conditions than Fed rate cuts.

**Commodity pricing.** Most global commodities are priced in dollars. Dollar strength mechanically suppresses commodity prices in dollar terms, hurting commodity exporters (most EM). Dollar weakness boosts them.

**Portfolio return mechanics.** A DM investor holding EM assets earns returns in two components: local currency asset return plus currency return. When the dollar strengthens, the currency return is negative, offsetting local gains. When the dollar weakens, currency return is positive, adding to local gains. This effect alone can add or subtract 5–15% per year.

**Reflexivity.** Dollar direction and capital flows are mutually reinforcing. The system has two meta-stable equilibria (strong dollar / DM dominance, and weak dollar / EM rotation) with non-linear transitions between them.

### Time Horizon

Full rotation cycle: 8–15 years. The DM dominance phase (strong dollar, DM outperformance) typically lasts 5–8 years. The EM rotation phase (weak dollar, EM outperformance) typically lasts 2–5 years. The asymmetry exists because DM has structural advantages (deeper capital markets, stronger governance, technology innovation) that make DM dominance the default state, while EM rotation requires specific catalysts.

## scope_limits

1. **Does not predict timing of catalyst arrival.** The theory identifies what the catalysts are (dollar weakening, China credit impulse turning positive) but cannot predict WHEN they will fire. The accumulation phase can persist 3–7 years.
2. **Treats EM as heterogeneous, not monolithic.** The theory does not claim all EM markets move together. China, India, Brazil, Korea, and Taiwan are different economies with different drivers. The regional sequencing is a prediction, not an assumption.
3. **Dollar direction is exogenous to this theory.** The theory does not itself explain WHY the dollar weakens — that causal explanation comes from other theories (fiscal dominance, debt cycle dynamics). This theory predicts what happens WHEN the dollar weakens.
4. **Magnitude predictions depend on China being investable.** If China becomes uninvestable for Western capital, the theory's rotation magnitude predictions collapse by 40–60%. A China-excluding rotation is possible but substantially smaller.
5. **Does not predict absolute DM declines during rotation.** Rotation is about relative performance. During 2003–2007, DM still delivered positive absolute returns; EM simply returned much more. The theory predicts EM outperformance, not DM losses.

## key_assumptions

1. **Capital seeks highest risk-adjusted returns across borders.** International capital is sufficiently mobile that large and persistent expected-return differentials attract reallocation. If capital mobility is structurally impaired (e.g., by sanctions, capital controls, or geopolitical fragmentation), the mechanism weakens.

2. **Valuation gaps mean-revert over full cycles.** The PE discount of EM relative to DM is cyclical, not permanent. This assumes EM governance, institutions, and growth potential are sufficient to justify roughly comparable long-run multiples. If EM structurally deserves lower multiples, the gap's persistence is rational, not a mispricing.

3. **The dollar cycle exists and is mean-reverting.** The dollar appreciates and depreciates in multi-year cycles. This has been true historically (1985–1995 decline, 1995–2002 appreciation, 2002–2008 decline, 2011–2024 appreciation). The assumption would break if the dollar enters a permanently strong regime — but 10+ years of continuous strengthening with no reversal has never occurred in the floating-rate era.

4. **China credit impulse transmits to global EM activity.** China's domestic credit cycle drives commodity demand, Asian supply chain activity, and EM earnings revisions with a 6–12 month lag. This assumption would break if China enters a Japan-style balance sheet recession where credit creation fails to transmit to real demand.

5. **The reflexive loop operates as described.** Capital inflows strengthen EM currencies, which eases financial conditions, which attracts more capital. This positive feedback has been operative in every historical EM rotation. It would break if EM central banks fully sterilise inflows or if capital controls prevent the feedback from operating.

## deep_falsifiers

| # | Condition | Logic |
|---|-----------|-------|
| H1 | EM PE discount widens to 60%+ and persists for 5+ years without ANY period of EM outperformance exceeding 6 months | If the valuation gap reaches historically unprecedented extremes AND persists without ANY rotation, the mean-reversion mechanism may be broken. Possible explanations: structural EM discount is justified (governance, rule of law, capital controls permanently impair EM returns), or DM has achieved genuine permanent growth superiority. This has NEVER occurred in the historical record — EM has always outperformed at some point following extreme PE discounts. The threshold is deliberately extreme. |
| H2 | China becomes genuinely uninvestable for Western capital (Taiwan conflict, comprehensive sanctions on Chinese financial system, or China imposes capital controls preventing foreign portfolio investment) | If China — which represents approximately 30% of broad EM indices and is the lead indicator for rotation — becomes uninvestable, the theory's magnitude predictions collapse. EM rotation without China is possible via India, Brazil, and SE Asia, but the magnitude is 40–60% smaller. This falsifier does not kill the CONCEPT of capital flow rotation but kills the MAGNITUDE predictions as currently specified. A China-excluding rotation module would need to be built separately. |
| H3 | Dollar strengthens for 10+ consecutive years while all other rotation conditions are present (EM PE discount exceeds 40%, China credit impulse has been positive in at least 3 of those years) | If the dollar strengthens persistently despite all conditions that should weaken it, the dollar gravity field is too strong for valuation arbitrage to overcome. The dollar's reserve status, safe haven premium, and DM growth superiority create a structural floor that rotation dynamics cannot breach. 10 years is a demanding threshold but justified: 5–8 year dollar strength cycles are normal. Only 10+ years with simultaneously favourable rotation conditions would constitute genuine falsification. |

## revision_triggers

1. **China enters a confirmed Japan-style balance sheet recession.** If China's credit impulse becomes structurally impotent (banks create credit but it fails to transmit to demand because households and corporates are repairing balance sheets), the causal mechanism at step 4 breaks. The theory would need revision to either: (a) remove China as the lead indicator and rebuild around India/non-China EM, or (b) reduce magnitude predictions by 40–60% and redefine regional sequencing.

2. **Geopolitical fragmentation structurally segments global capital markets.** If the world bifurcates into mutually exclusive US-allied and China-allied blocs with restricted cross-bloc capital flows, the "capital seeks highest returns across borders" assumption breaks. The theory would need revision to operate within blocs rather than across them.

3. **A new reserve architecture eliminates the dollar cycle.** If a credible alternative settlement and reserve system emerges (digital currencies, multi-currency baskets) that prevents the dollar from experiencing multi-year appreciation/depreciation cycles, the primary catalyst mechanism disappears.

4. **Three consecutive rotation attempts are aborted by exogenous shocks.** The 2017–2018 experience (trade war aborted a rotation with strong fundamental support) is one instance. If this becomes a pattern — catalysts fire, rotation begins, then exogenous force aborts it repeatedly — the theory's predictive value for actionable trading degrades even if the underlying mechanism is correct.
