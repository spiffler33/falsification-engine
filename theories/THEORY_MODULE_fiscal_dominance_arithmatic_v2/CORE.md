# CORE.md — Fiscal Dominance: Devaluation Arithmetic

*Theory: fiscal_dominance_arithmetic*
*Stability class: persistent*
*Last updated: April 2026*

---

## theory_id

`fiscal_dominance_arithmetic`

---

## core_claim

The US federal government's debt has reached a level where the arithmetic of interest expense relative to tax receipts is self-reinforcing and politically irresolvable through austerity or default. The only historically consistent resolution is devaluation — sustained negative real interest rates that erode the real value of nominal claims. Hard assets (those with scarcity or replacement cost) outperform nominal claims on a multi-year basis during such episodes. The devaluation occurs through the path of least political resistance: no explicit policy choice is required, inflation is diffuse, and nominal prices mask the real wealth transfer from creditors to debtors.

---

## causal_mechanism

### The Chain

1. The sovereign has accumulated debt at a scale where interest payments consume a large and rising share of tax receipts. The debt stock exists. It must be serviced — interest payments are legally obligatory, senior to all other spending in practice.

2. The weighted average interest rate on this debt is rising. Debt issued during prior low-rate periods matures and rolls into current market rates. Each quarter, the effective rate on total debt ratchets higher as low-rate debt is replaced by high-rate debt. This process is automatic — no policy action required.

3. Interest expense becomes the fastest-growing line item in the federal budget. Unlike discretionary spending, it cannot be "cut" without defaulting. It competes with defense, entitlements, and all other obligations for the same revenue base.

4. The deficit is structural, not cyclical. It persists during economic expansion, meaning it cannot shrink when the economy improves — the economy is already performing reasonably. It will widen in recession (automatic stabilizers, stimulus, lower tax receipts).

5. Three mathematically possible resolutions exist:

   (a) **Austerity** — cut spending or raise taxes enough to stabilize the debt trajectory. Requires politically extraordinary action: cutting entitlements, defense, or raising taxes dramatically. No democracy at comparable debt ratios has voluntarily imposed sufficient austerity. Both major parties propose expansions. Probability: very low.

   (b) **Default / restructuring** — fail to make interest payments. Would destroy the sovereign bond market, the currency's reserve status, and the financial system that depends on both. Catastrophic and irrational when option (c) exists. Probability: negligible barring extreme political dysfunction.

   (c) **Devaluation** — reduce the real value of the debt by allowing inflation to exceed the interest rate on the debt. Every major sovereign at comparable debt levels has chosen this path. It is the path of least political resistance because no explicit vote is taken, inflation is diffuse, nominal wages and asset prices rise (masking the transfer), and the government — as the largest debtor — benefits most. The mechanism: keep nominal interest rates below nominal GDP growth (and below inflation) for an extended period. The real debt burden shrinks each year by the gap between inflation and interest rates.

6. Devaluation produces specific asset price consequences. Assets denominated in nominal terms (sovereign bonds, corporate bonds, cash) lose real purchasing power even if nominal value is preserved. Assets with scarcity or replacement cost (commodities, real assets, equities with pricing power) maintain or increase real value as their prices adjust upward with inflation.

7. The speed of devaluation matters for expression but not for the directional claim. Slow devaluation (sustained moderate inflation with rates held below) produces gradual real wealth transfer. Fast devaluation (inflation spike) produces rapid repricing. The speed depends on central bank behavior, which is exogenous to this theory.

8. A secondary accelerant: non-dollar energy and trade settlement reduces structural demand for the currency, reinforcing the devaluation pressure. This mechanism is slower-moving than the domestic arithmetic and is primarily owned by the `monetary_architecture` theory — but it directionally reinforces the devaluation trajectory.

9. The feedback loop. Devaluation worsens the arithmetic in the short run if rates stay high (higher inflation leads the central bank to keep rates elevated, increasing interest expense on rolling debt). The loop only closes if the central bank accommodates — keeps rates below inflation. The fiscal arithmetic makes accommodation inevitable because the alternative (fighting inflation with higher rates) increases the deficit through interest expense. This is the trap.

### Time Horizon

Primary: 3–10 years. The rollover of low-rate debt to high-rate debt takes 5–7 years to complete (weighted average maturity of outstanding sovereign debt is approximately 6 years). The devaluation trajectory is measured in years, not quarters.

Secondary: Asset price implications begin pricing in immediately once the market recognizes the trajectory. The question of how much is already discounted is a tactical assessment, not a theory question.

### Distinction from fiscal_dominance_liquidity

The liquidity module describes the FLOW mechanism — how deficit spending creates reserves and drives asset prices quarter to quarter (1–6 month horizon). This module describes the STOCK problem — the cumulative debt trajectory and the mathematical endpoint it implies. Liquidity can be temporarily inactive (one quarter of deficit narrowing) while the arithmetic remains untenable. The arithmetic is a slower-moving, higher-conviction signal.

## scope_limits

1. Does not predict timing of market recognition or devaluation acceleration.
2. Does not predict the speed of devaluation (slow grind vs. fast spike) — speed depends on central bank behavior, which is exogenous to this theory.
3. Applies specifically to US fiscal conditions; other sovereigns face different arithmetic with different reserve-status buffers.
4. Does not predict dollar collapse or hyperinflation — the theory predicts gradual erosion of purchasing power within a functioning monetary system.
5. Does not distinguish between devaluation channels (CPI inflation, asset inflation, currency depreciation) — the channel depends on monetary policy response.
6. Does not, by itself, specify the best trade vehicle — the choice among gold, inflation-protected bonds, commodities, or pricing-power equities belongs in the expression layer, not the invariant theory.

## key_assumptions

1. **Political economy of democracies.** No democratic sovereign at comparable debt-to-GDP has voluntarily imposed austerity sufficient to resolve the arithmetic. Both major parties currently propose fiscal expansion, not consolidation. This assumption is load-bearing: if a credible bipartisan deficit reduction framework emerges and is sustained, the theory weakens materially.

2. **Central bank accommodation is ultimately forced.** The central bank will accommodate (keep rates below inflation) because fighting inflation with higher rates increases the deficit through interest expense, creating a fiscal spiral. The alternative — sustained high rates that collapse the sovereign bond market — is institutional suicide for the central bank. This assumption could break if the central bank demonstrates willingness to impose severe recession to defend price stability regardless of fiscal consequences.

3. **Reserve currency status delays but does not prevent.** Dollar reserve status provides a buffer: the sovereign can borrow in its own currency, foreign demand for the currency provides structural support, and the central bank can monetize without immediate inflationary consequences. This extends the timeline but does not change the directional conclusion. If reserve status erodes (a process owned by `monetary_architecture`), the timeline compresses.

4. **Rollover arithmetic is mechanical.** Existing debt matures on a known schedule. The weighted average coupon below market rate means interest expense rises automatically as debt rolls. This is the most mechanically certain component of the theory — deterministic given the maturity profile and current rates.

5. **Devaluation is the historical precedent.** The 1940s–1950s US episode (debt/GDP from 120% to 60% via sustained negative real rates), the 1946–1974 UK episode, and multiple EM precedents all resolved via devaluation. The US has more room (reserve currency, deep markets) but the historical pattern is consistent.

## deep_falsifiers

These conditions, if met, would indicate that the fiscal arithmetic devaluation mechanism is NOT the dominant trajectory. No severity is assigned here — severity is a scoring parameter in ACTIVATION.md.

| ID | Condition | Logic |
|----|-----------|-------|
| H1 | **Genuine fiscal consolidation sustained.** The deficit falls below a manageable level for 4+ consecutive quarters through actual spending cuts or tax increases — not one-time items, accounting changes, or seasonal tax-receipt lump sums. | If the government demonstrates sustained ability to reduce the deficit below the level where arithmetic pressure builds, the "no political will" assumption is falsified. The 4-quarter requirement eliminates seasonal noise and one-off adjustments. This has not occurred since the late 1990s surplus (driven by an unrepeatable combination of the dot-com boom, defense cuts, and capital gains windfalls). |
| H2 | **Interest/receipts ratio declines to a manageable level sustained for 4+ quarters.** The critical ratio improves materially through some combination of lower rates, faster revenue growth, or slower debt accumulation — and holds. | If the ratio that drives the theory (interest expense as a share of tax receipts) improves to historically manageable levels and stays there, the arithmetic pressure eases. The improvement must be sustained, not a one-quarter artifact of tax deadline receipts. |
| H3 | **Productivity-driven real GDP growth above 4% sustained for 3+ years.** A genuine productivity revolution drives sustained elevated real growth, causing tax receipts to grow faster than interest expense organically. | This would represent the "grow out of the debt" scenario. If it occurs, the devaluation prediction is materially weakened because the denominator (GDP, receipts) is growing fast enough to manage the numerator (debt, interest). This would be historically extraordinary — the US has not sustained this growth rate for 12 quarters since the 1960s — but it is theoretically possible (AI or equivalent technological shift). |

## stability_class

**Persistent.** The core claim — that the arithmetic of US federal debt forces devaluation as the resolution of least political resistance — is structural and unlikely to toggle within 5 years. The arithmetic is self-reinforcing: higher debt generates higher interest expense, which increases the deficit, which adds more debt. The political economy of democratic resistance to austerity is a deep structural feature, not a cyclical variable.

---

## revision_triggers

These would constitute genuine revisions to the invariant theory, not threshold recalibrations or tactical updates:

1. **A genuine fiscal surplus sustained for multiple years** — not a one-time windfall but evidence that the political economy assumption has changed.
2. **A structural change in political willingness to impose austerity** — e.g., a constitutional balanced-budget mechanism with enforcement teeth, or a bipartisan grand bargain that durably reduces the deficit trajectory.
3. **A new monetary architecture that resolves sovereign debt without devaluation** — e.g., perpetual zero-coupon bonds accepted by markets, or a technology-enabled reduction in the cost of government that structurally reduces deficits.
4. **Evidence that reserve currency status permanently immunizes against fiscal arithmetic** — if the US sustains extreme interest/receipts ratios for a full decade without any devaluation transmission, the "reserve currency delays but does not prevent" assumption would need revision.

## historical_episodes_referenced

These provide the evidentiary base for the theory and are included for auditability:

- **1942–1951 US financial repression.** Debt/GDP peaked at 120%, resolved via sustained negative real rates. CPI ran 8–14% with rates capped at 2.5%. Debt/GDP halved to approximately 60% by 1960 without nominal default. The template for devaluation as resolution.
- **1946–1974 UK post-war fiscal dominance.** Debt/GDP 250% at peak, resolved via 30 years of sustained negative real rates and periodic sterling devaluations. The pound lost approximately 75% of purchasing power. The most extended devaluation episode by a major sovereign.
- **1970s US stagflation.** Fiscal deficits plus oil shock plus loose monetary policy. Debt/GDP only approximately 35% — much lower than today. Despite much lower debt levels, the fiscal-inflation dynamic produced dramatic hard asset outperformance.
- **EM precedents (Brazil 1990s–2000s, Turkey 2010s–2020s, Argentina persistent).** More extreme trajectories but the same mechanism: interest expense exceeds capacity to service, currency devalues, hard assets outperform nominal claims. The US has more room because of dollar reserve status, but the direction is the same.
