# CORE.md — Valuation Mean Reversion & Margin of Safety

*theory_id: `valuation_mean_reversion`*
*stability_class: **persistent** — the arithmetic relationship between purchase price and forward returns has held across 120+ years of data. The core mechanism is mathematical, not contingent on policy regime, market structure, or technology epoch.*

---

## core_claim

Asset prices mean-revert toward levels justified by the cash flows they generate. When the price paid per unit of earnings is high, forward real returns are mechanically low — you are borrowing returns from the future. The equity risk premium (earnings yield minus risk-free yield) is the single most important measure of this: when it compresses toward zero, equities offer negligible compensation for bearing equity risk, and the magnitude of any drawdown triggered by an external catalyst is amplified by the distance between current price and fair value.

---

## causal_mechanism

1. Asset prices rise over an extended period, driven by some combination of earnings growth, multiple expansion, passive inflows, momentum, and narrative.

2. The price of the asset detaches from the cash flow it generates. This is arithmetic, not opinion: price equals earnings times multiple. If price rises faster than earnings, the multiple expands. A higher multiple means more dollars paid per dollar of earnings, which means lower forward returns, mechanically.

3. The opportunity cost of equity ownership becomes measurable. When the risk-free alternative pays as much or more than the equity earnings yield, a rational allocator has no incentive to bear equity risk. The only remaining argument for equities is growth — but the growth must be sufficient to compensate for the valuation premium. At extreme multiples, the required growth rate is implausibly high.

4. This state can persist for years. The mechanism does not predict timing. Persistence is driven by momentum and passive inflows (buyers regardless of price), career risk (being out of a rising market), narrative ("this time is different"), and fiscal liquidity providing a continuous bid under assets.

5. Resolution arrives through one of three channels:
   - **Price decline** (most common): A catalyst causes repricing. The drawdown magnitude is proportional to the valuation excess — the distance from current price to fair value.
   - **Earnings growth** (the soft landing): Earnings grow fast enough that the multiple normalizes without price decline. Requires either a genuine productivity revolution or substantial inflation (nominal earnings grow while real multiple compresses). Takes 5–10 years.
   - **Inflationary grind**: Nominal prices tread water or rise slowly while inflation erodes real value. The multiple compresses in real terms without a nominal crash. Forward real returns are poor for a decade. Investors do not "lose money" nominally but lose purchasing power.

6. Post-resolution, the equity risk premium is restored — either through lower prices, higher earnings, or lower real values. Forward returns become attractive again. This is the deployment signal.

**Sectoral margin of safety principle:** Market-level overvaluation does not preclude sector-level opportunities. Even in expensive markets, individual sectors rotate through periods of temporary distress that create margin of safety. The process: identify sectors where the business model is intact and cash flows will recover, but price reflects temporary (not permanent) headwinds. Different sector types have different appropriate valuation anchors — book value for leveraged balance sheets, replacement cost for tangible-asset producers, normalized earnings for cyclicals.

## scope_limits

1. **Does not predict timing.** The theory identifies the level from which a correction starts and the magnitude of the correction, not the date. Being early is indistinguishable from being wrong. Any hypothesis making a timing claim must cite another theory for the catalyst.

2. **Predicts magnitude conditional on catalyst from another theory.** Valuation excess determines HOW FAR prices fall; the catalyst (recession, credit event, fragility break) determines WHEN. Neither dimension is sufficient alone.

3. **Does not claim "sell everything."** Valuations can be extreme for years while equities rise further. The theory says forward returns are POOR, not that prices cannot go higher first. There is a crucial difference between "expensive" and "imminent crash."

4. **Applies primarily to US public equity markets.** The empirical calibration is against US data since 1900. International application uses relative-value logic (US premium vs. alternatives) rather than absolute thresholds, which have different ranges in different markets.

5. **Does not distinguish resolution channels on its own.** Which of the three resolution channels (price decline, earnings growth, inflationary grind) is operative depends on interactions with other theories — particularly the fiscal dominance and debt cycle modules.

---

## key_assumptions

1. **Cyclically adjusted earnings are a valid anchor for equity pricing.** If reported earnings are permanently elevated above historical norms (due to technology, monopoly power, or accounting changes), the degree of overvaluation is smaller than the theory claims.

2. **Profit margins mean-revert over a full business cycle.** Multiples applied to above-average margins produce double overvaluation. If margins are structurally and permanently higher, the effective overvaluation is less severe.

3. **The equity risk premium framework is the correct pricing model.** Equities compete with bonds for capital. If a different framework (e.g., equities priced purely on growth optionality, independent of bond yields) becomes operative, the ERP signal loses relevance.

4. **Historical forward-return relationships from valuation starting points hold in the future.** The 120-year record shows that entry at high multiples produces low forward returns. This assumes no permanent structural break in the relationship between purchase price and subsequent returns.

5. **Cash or short-duration bonds serve as a viable alternative when the ERP is compressed.** If financial repression makes cash a guaranteed loser in real terms, the "hold cash and wait" recommendation breaks down even when equities are expensive.

## deep_falsifiers

These conditions would kill the theory itself — not a hypothesis derived from it.

| # | Condition | Logic |
|---|-----------|-------|
| H1 | Forward 10-year real returns from extreme multiples (cyclically adjusted PE above 30) exceed 7% annualized | If buying at extreme multiples delivers above-average real returns, high prices are NOT borrowing from the future. The entire arithmetic of mean reversion would be wrong. This has never occurred in the 120+ year record. |
| H2 | Equity risk premium remains below zero for 10+ years without any drawdown exceeding 20% | If equities deliver adequate total returns despite offering no risk premium over cash for a full decade, the ERP framework is not the correct pricing model. Equities would be priced on growth optionality alone, and the earnings-yield-vs-bond-yield comparison would be irrelevant. |
| H3 | Profit margins do not revert over a full business cycle — net margins stay above 11% through a complete recession without declining below 10% | Margin mean reversion is load-bearing for the valuation argument (reported PE understates normalized PE when margins are elevated). If margins are structurally and permanently higher — surviving even a recession without compressing — the effective overvaluation is materially smaller than the theory claims. |

---

## revision_triggers

- A sustained period (10+ years) where the historical relationship between starting CAPE and forward returns breaks down would require re-examining the core claim.
- Evidence that passive investment flows have permanently altered the mean to which valuations revert (i.e., the "fair value" CAPE is structurally higher than the historical 17 average) would require recalibrating the mechanism, though not necessarily invalidating it.
- A successful structural argument that equity duration has permanently lengthened (e.g., because intangible assets generate longer-lived cash flows than tangible assets did), justifying permanently higher multiples, would require revising key_assumptions #1 and #2.
- If a new asset class emerges that dominates the role of equities in portfolios, making the ERP comparison between equities and bonds less relevant to capital allocation decisions.
